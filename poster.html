Dual Exposure to Maternity Care Deserts and Food Insecurity: Relationships With Adverse Birth Outcomes

Drexton Andrews & Jayla Callahan

Research Center for Rural, Child, and Family Health · Tuskegee University · Tuskegee, Alabama 36088

Systematic literature review

Significance of the Problem

Rural south-central Alabama counties have experienced hospital closures and uneven distribution of clinical services. Large shares of counties meet criteria for maternity care deserts (limited or no obstetric hospitals and specialists), while household food insecurity and retail food environments with limited healthy-food access register above national comparison levels. Long travel to clinics and stores, fewer scheduled prenatal visits, and uneven retail food availability can coincide in the same geography.

  • Geographic loss of obstetric services is linked to worse pregnancy-related outcomes where birthing hospitals and specialists are absent or distant (March of Dimes, 2024; Atwani et al., 2025).
  • Driving-time and distance to obstetric services predict selected adverse outcomes in rural mapping studies (Sugg et al., 2025).
  • Food insecurity and neighborhood food access during pregnancy are linked to gestational diabetes, stress-related morbidity, and selected birth outcomes in recent U.S. and international syntheses (Bell et al., 2024; Aris et al., 2024; Rampersad et al., 2024).
  • Independent literatures rarely integrate dual exposure (care access + nutrition insecurity) within the same county-level geography.

Systematic Review Question

What is the relationship between dual exposure to maternity care deserts and food insecurity on adverse birth outcomes among pregnant residents in rural south-central Alabama, and what structural and occupational interventions could reduce the combined effects of these exposures?

Sub-aims: (1) synthesize peer-reviewed evidence from the last five years on each exposure and perinatal outcomes; (2) describe how distance to obstetric services, fragmented prenatal care pathways, and food-access constraints may compound when both exposures co-occur (Metz et al., 2022; Sugg et al., 2025); (3) catalog policy, health system, and workforce interventions applicable to rural Alabama counties in the review scope.

Review of Literature

Maternity care access and outcomes. Deserts reflect hospital obstetric unit closures, uneven geographic distribution of obstetric specialists, and long driving distances to birthing facilities. A recent U.S. county-level analysis associates desert status with higher maternal and pregnancy-related mortality versus full-access counties (Atwani et al., 2025). March of Dimes reports document widespread Alabama counties with no or insufficient birthing services (March of Dimes, 2024).

Food insecurity and pregnancy. A 2024 meta-analysis of high-income countries reports associations between pregnancy food insecurity and gestational diabetes, mood disorders, and stress-related outcomes (Bell et al., 2024). U.S. national cohort work links self-reported food insecurity and neighborhood food access metrics to birth outcomes (Aris et al., 2024). NHIS-based analyses tie food insecurity to hypertensive disorders of pregnancy (Rampersad et al., 2024).

Structural context. Rural–urban differences in hypertensive disorders of pregnancy underscore the importance of place-based risk and access patterns over time (Metz et al., 2022). Fine-grained work on driving distance to obstetric services links desert geography to perinatal indicators in comparable rural U.S. settings (Sugg et al., 2025). March of Dimes reporting characterizes widespread Alabama counties with limited birthing services (March of Dimes, 2024).

Overlapping exposures in evidence. Recent syntheses tie food insecurity during pregnancy to stress, metabolic complications, and patterns of prenatal service use (Bell et al., 2024; Aris et al., 2024), supporting a dual-exposure lens that combines maternity access and nutrition security.

Summary of Evidence

  • Parallel bodies of evidence link maternity access deficits and food insecurity to adverse pregnancy outcomes in population-level studies; direct studies of joint exposure in rural south-central Alabama remain limited.
  • Plausible compounded pathways include delayed diagnosis of hypertension and glucose dysregulation when prenatal visits are infrequent; nutritional shortfalls and stress-related physiologic load; longer transport times to obstetric units; and scheduling and distance-related delays affecting both food procurement and prenatal visit attendance.
  • Co-occurring facility and food conditions: where maternity care deserts align with low neighborhood food access or elevated household food insecurity, the literature documents added logistical hurdles to screening and treatment (March of Dimes, 2024; Sugg et al., 2025).
  • Intervention evidence is strongest for discrete strategies (e.g., telehealth monitoring, food-benefit expansion, perinatal workforce programs) and less often evaluated as bundled “desert + food” packages — a gap this review highlights for policy and research.

Recommended Directions (From Synthesis)

  • Structural: stabilize obstetric capacity (hospital partnerships, rural OB stipends), expand Medicaid coverage duration, invest in grocery and SNAP/WIC access points, and fund transportation solutions tied to prenatal schedules.
  • Occupational / clinical: community health worker navigation, group prenatal care where feasible, standardized food insecurity screening with referral, telehealth for hypertension and diabetes surveillance, and continuing education on respectful maternity care.
  • Research: prospective studies or state-linked data that code both desert status and validated food-security measures for birthing residents of target rural Alabama counties, with clear outcome definitions (e.g., HDP, preterm birth, severe maternal morbidity).

Review Protocol (Methods)

  • Databases: PubMed/MEDLINE, CINAHL, Embase, Scopus (and Cochrane Library for prior reviews).
  • Concepts: maternity care deserts / obstetric unit closure / maternity care access; food insecurity / food desert / food environment; pregnancy or perinatal outcomes; rural Alabama / U.S. South / comparable rural regions (for contextual comparators).
  • Limits: English language; peer-reviewed publications from the last five years (e.g., 2022–2026, finalized at data lock); human studies.
  • Inclusion: empirical studies (quantitative, qualitative, mixed-methods) and reviews that address at least one exposure (maternity access or food insecurity / food environment) and birth or maternal morbidity outcomes, or interventions targeting access and/or nutrition in pregnancy.
  • Exclusion: editorials without data, single-case reports, studies with no pregnancy-related outcomes.
  • Process: dual independent title/abstract and full-text screening, standardized data extraction, narrative synthesis grouped by exposure, population, and outcome, with study quality appraised using design-appropriate tools.

Implementation Plan (Knowledge to Action)

  • Translate synthesis into a brief for stakeholders: state perinatal quality collaboratives, FQHC networks, and partners in the study counties.
  • Prioritize intervention bundles that pair clinical access (telehealth BP cuffs, mobile ultrasound or referral contracts) with nutrition action (WIC outreach, SNAP enrollment assistance, produce prescription pilots).
  • Align measures with Healthy People 2030 maternal-infant objectives for tracking changes over time.
  • Pursue partnerships for linked administrative data analyses if primary studies remain sparse.

Evaluation Plan

  • Review documentation: retain search strings and decision logs; tabulate included studies by design, exposure definition, and outcome.
  • Synthesis limits: document limited dual-exposure estimates for birthing cohorts in rural south-central Alabama.
  • Intervention metrics (for programs): prenatal care initiation and adequacy; third-trimester ED visits for hypertension; food-security screen positivity with referral completion; preterm birth and HDP rates in service catchments (where data sharing permits).
  • Long term: advocate for research funding for prospective cohorts or natural experiments (e.g., hospital reopening, new grocery investment) with pregnancy cohorts.
